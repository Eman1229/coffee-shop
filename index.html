OUR HISTORY

The history of coffee begins in Ethiopia, where, according to legend, a goat herder named Kaldi discovered the energizing effects of coffee berries in the 9th century. After noticing that his goats became lively after eating the berries, Kaldi tried them himself and shared the discovery with a local monastery. The monks began brewing the berries into a drink to stay awake during long prayers, and this practice gradually spread across Ethiopia and into the Arabian Peninsula.

By the 15th century, coffee was cultivated in Yemen, where it became an integral part of Sufi religious practices. The Sufis used coffee to remain alert during their nighttime prayers, and soon coffee spread throughout the Arab world. Coffeehouses, or "qahveh khaneh," emerged in cities like Mecca and Medina, becoming centers of cultural and intellectual exchange. The drink became popular in the Ottoman Empire, Persia, and North Africa, where it was brewed and consumed in various styles, such as Turkish coffee.

Coffee reached Europe in the 17th century, where it initially faced skepticism but soon gained widespread popularity. The first European coffeehouse opened in Venice in 1645, followed by London’s famous "Penny Universities" in the mid-1600s, where people gathered to discuss politics, literature, and philosophy. By the late 1600s, coffee had also spread to the Americas, where European colonists established coffee plantations in the Caribbean and South America, further cementing coffee as a global commodity. Today, coffee is one of the most consumed beverages worldwide, enjoyed in countless forms and styles across cultures.
